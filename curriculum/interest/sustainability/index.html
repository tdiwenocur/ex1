Sustainability Design

  • DES 40A — Energy, Materials, and Design Over Time
  • Global history of design across time, viewed through the lens of the effects of the creation and discovery of new energy sources, processes and materials on design.
  • DES 70 — Introduction to Textiles
  • Introduction to diverse methods for creating textile structures. Exploration of the creative potential of hand-constructed textiles, manipulation of fabric to create dimensional surfaces, and the basics of building and joining fabric structures.
  • DES 116 — Visual Communication: Graphic Design Studio
  • Multiple, conceptually-linked assignments focusing on the fundamental choices designers make in translating concepts into effective graphic form. Problem finding and analysis of audience needs. Design process from research and initial concepts to project prototypes.
  • DES 127A — Sustainable Design
  • Principles, practice and materials of contemporary sustainable design in the context of environmental crisis. History of sustainable design in relation to the fields of textiles, visual communication, interior architecture, exhibition design and lighting.
  • DES 127B — Studio Practice in Sustainable Design
  • Analysis and practice of sustainable design within studio context. Design project that incorporate the reuse of post consumer waste; standard materials vs. sustainable materials; Cradle to Cradle philosophy and practice. Field trips required.
  • DES 128 — Studio Practice in Sustainable Design
  • How is biology influencing architecture and design? This advanced undergraduate/graduate seminar examines recent biological theories and howthey are shaping design theory and practice and being used by artists to challenge us to think critically about these technologies and their outcomes. Examples will come from design, architecture, and art; theo­ries include pattern formation, morphogenesis, artificial life, genetic and tissue engineering, evolutionary programming, self-organization and emergence, social insects, biomimicry, and ecological design.
  • DES 131 — Global Fashion and Product Design
  • Exploration of materials, embellishments, and structural techniques derived from historic and contemporary world cultures. Emphasis on unique qualities of individual expression applied to hand made textiles, fashion and textile products.
  • DES 138 — Materials and Methods in Interior Design
  • Introduction to the finish materials used for interior design with special emphasis on sustainable and recycled products. Performance factors, relative costs and energy impacts, installation conditions and construction details, and design potential for a full range of interior materials.
  • DES 155A — Pattern, Form, and Surface
  • Experimental approaches to form-making through an examination of pattern, form, and surface in historical and contemporary contexts. Explorations of alternative design processes, methods, and materials that open up new possibilities for content creation and invention in design practice.
  • DES 170 — Experimental Fashion and Textile Design
  • Experimental approaches to fashion and textile design. Emphasis on developing conceptual ideas and translating them into one-of-a-kind garments and soft products. Exploration of a variety of current topics including sustainability, pattern design, new technologies, and social activism.

FAQ:

Why doesn’t the UC Davis Design Department not have focuses/tracks?

With all the fields of design that are quickly emerging, having a few focuses or tracks can be very limiting for design students now. Also, having a more open program allows students to explore different kinds of design without putting off graduation. It allows for a more personally tailored major for everyone in the program.

Why does the major start with a theoretical, foundational class like Design 1?

The UC Davis Design major is loosely based off of the Bauhaus school from the early 20th century. The Design 1 course is meant as a foundational course, introducing the basics of design thinking, design history, and a little bit of design practice. It’s an introduction so that people who might be interested in design can see if it’s something they want to pursue.


What is the purpose of Curriculum Navigator?

The curriculum navigator is designed to help students, new or otherwise, figure out what kinds of classes they might be interested in taking based on their design interests and tools or skills they want to cultivate.
